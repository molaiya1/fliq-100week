Behavioral Economics · Impulsivity Research
Impulsive financial decision-making in adolescence is the single strongest predictor of adult financial instability — stronger than income, education level, or family wealth.
Longitudinal research tracking cohorts from age 10 through adulthood found that adolescents who demonstrated low delay-of-gratification in low-stakes financial scenarios were significantly more likely to carry high-interest debt, have no emergency savings, and experience financial stress as adults — regardless of eventual income level.
Moffitt, T.E. et al. "A gradient of childhood self-control predicts health, wealth, and public safety." PNAS, 2011.
WWK Design Response
The FLIQ Score™ Impulse Control (IC) domain directly measures this behavioral pattern across five decision scenarios. Identifying IC profiles early — before adolescents develop fixed spending habits — is the entire premise of The $100 Week™ as a behavioral assessment tool, not just a simulation.

Wealth Gap Research · Racial Equity
Black and Hispanic households are 2–3× more likely to have zero emergency savings and 40–60% less likely to hold investment accounts — gaps that originate in financial behavior patterns established before age 16.
The Federal Reserve's Survey of Consumer Finances consistently shows that the racial wealth gap is driven not exclusively by income disparity but by differential access to financial education, behavioral modeling, and early savings habits. Communities without early intervention show compounding disadvantage across generations.
Federal Reserve Board. "Survey of Consumer Finances." Triennial survey, most recent 2022. Board of Governors of the Federal Reserve System.

Financial Education Research · Program Efficacy
Simulation-based financial learning produces significantly stronger behavioral transfer than knowledge-based instruction alone — students retain and apply decision-making strategies at 2–3× the rate.
Studies comparing didactic financial instruction with experiential/simulation-based approaches consistently find that participants in simulation conditions show higher rates of real-world behavior change — including opening savings accounts, tracking spending, and demonstrating delay-of-gratification — in the 6 and 12 months following intervention. The mechanism is behavioral rehearsal: students practice decisions under low-stakes conditions before encountering them in the real world.
Fernandes, D., Lynch, J.G., & Netemeyer, R.G. "Financial Literacy, Financial Education, and Downstream Financial Behaviors." Management Science, 2014.
WWK Design Response
The $100 Week™ is not a quiz. It is a behavioral rehearsal environment — five days of consequential decisions where the outcome (ending balance, FLIQ profile) depends entirely on how students actually behave, not how they perform on a test. This is the specific mechanism the literature identifies as producing durable behavioral change.

Behavioral Science · Self-Regulation Development
Adolescents who can accurately identify their own financial decision-making patterns show significantly better financial outcomes at 5-year follow-up — regardless of whether those patterns were initially "good" or "bad."
Self-awareness of behavioral tendencies — knowing that you are an impulsive spender, a risk-averse saver, or a social follower — is a stronger predictor of improved financial behavior than receiving instruction about the "right" behaviors. The act of accurate self-identification creates the cognitive gap that motivates change. This is the basis for behavioral profiling interventions in youth financial education.
Lusardi, A. & Mitchell, O.S. "The Economic Importance of Financial Literacy: Theory and Evidence." Journal of Economic Literature, 2014.
WWK Design Response
The FLIQ Score™ is designed as a self-awareness tool first. It does not tell students what they should have done — it shows them how they actually behaved across five decision contexts. The nine behavioral profile types (Financial Starter through Exceptional) are descriptive, not prescriptive. The training — Financial Foundations™ curriculum delivered by iMPACT Partners™ — comes after the profile is established, when behavioral self-awareness creates the opening for instruction.

The WWK Philosophy — Evidence-Based Design
We don't teach kids what to think.
We show them how they think.

The most important design decision in the WealthWise Kids™ ecosystem is what we don't do: we don't tell students which financial decisions are right or wrong while they're making them. The simulation captures raw, uninfluenced behavioral responses — what students actually do under pressure, not what they think they should say.

This is not a philosophical preference. The research is unambiguous: behavioral interventions that measure authentic decision-making before delivering instruction produce significantly better outcomes than programs that signal correct answers from the start. Self-identification of behavioral patterns — not external instruction — is the mechanism that drives lasting change.

The training lives in Financial Foundations™, delivered by credentialed iMPACT Partners™ after the FLIQ profile is established. The $100 Week™ is the diagnostic. The curriculum is the intervention. The data is the throughline connecting both.

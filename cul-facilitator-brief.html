FLIQ Score — What Each Dimension Means in a Career Context
I
Impulse Control
How often a student chooses the immediate reward over the longer-term benefit when both options are present.
Career signal: Do they hold out for a better offer, or take the first one? Can they resist lifestyle inflation when a first real paycheck arrives?
P
Planning
Whether a student thinks ahead about competing priorities — transit, timing, tradeoffs — before committing to a choice.
Career signal: Do they show up prepared? Handle a conflict between class, practice, and an interview? Think before they act?
R
Risk Awareness
How accurately a student evaluates uncertainty — whether they account for what could go wrong before acting.
Career signal: Do they understand that "I'll pay you back" has a track record? Do they read the situation before committing?
R
Resilience
Whether a student adapts when a plan falls apart — finding an alternative rather than shutting down or spending impulsively.
Career signal: When something doesn't go as expected on the job — do they problem-solve or freeze? This is the trait employers can't teach.
Debrief Guide — Full Group (20 minutes)
"Which decision was the hardest for you? Why?"
Opens the room. Most students will say Day 3 (interview). Let them name the real pressure — transit, money, not wanting to ask for help.
"Did anyone change their mind before hitting confirm? What made you go back?"
Surfaces planning vs. impulse live. The ones who changed their mind have high Planning scores — they just don't know it yet.
"Day 4 — side hustle money. What did you choose, and what was your logic?"
The reinvest/reserve/reward ratio is a business concept most adults get wrong. Celebrate whoever saw the reinvest option as the smart play.
"If you had $400 instead of $100, would you have made the same choices?"
This is the most important question. Most say no — which reveals that their behavior is a function of scarcity, not character. That reframe matters.
"Look at your FLIQ Score. Which dimension surprised you most?"
Connects data to self-awareness. Follow with: "What would you want to change about that score before you start a job?"
When Students Ask...
"Is a higher score better?"
"The score is a pattern, not a grade. High Impulse Control means you hold back. Low means you act fast. Both matter — it just depends on the situation. Today you got data. Most adults never do."
"What does this mean for my career?"
"It means you now have data on how you make decisions under pressure. That's what every employer is trying to figure out in an interview. You figured it out first — before you walked into one."
"Can I retake it to get a better score?"
"You can — but the score will just reflect whatever you choose. The point isn't to score high. The point is to understand how you actually think, not how you think you should think."

What This Means — Career Readiness Context
Impulse Control (IC)
The ability to pause before acting under financial pressure. In career and adult life settings, students with strong IC scores manage responses under stress, avoid reactive spending, and make deliberate decisions when stakes are high.
Planning Orientation (PO)
The tendency to set goals and work toward them systematically. Strong PO scores predict savings behavior, goal-setting consistency, and a student's ability to defer short-term gratification for long-term gain.
Risk Awareness (RA)
The depth of evaluation before taking on financial risk. Students with high RA scores read the fine print, ask clarifying questions before committing, and are less likely to fall victim to predatory financial products.
Resilience & Bounce-Back (RB)
Response to financial setback and unexpected loss. High RB scores indicate a student's capacity to recover, recalibrate, and continue making sound decisions after a financial disruption — a critical life skill.
Recommended Next Steps
1
Debrief With Students
Share class-level FLIQ results with students without revealing individual scores. Facilitate a group conversation around the weakest class trait — what decisions led there, and what they'd do differently.
2
Pre/Post Design for Fall
The most powerful use of FLIQ data is a delta: run The $100 Week™ at program intake, deliver financial capability programming, then run it again at semester end. The score change is your proof of behavioral impact.
3
Prescribe by FLIQ Profile
Each FLIQ profile maps to a specific curriculum pathway. Students with low IC scores benefit from structured pause-and-reflect exercises. Students with low PO benefit from goal-setting and savings modeling.
4
Expand to Full Cohort
This pilot covers the college readiness track. Bringing FLIQ intake into all CUL youth programs (ages 11–18) creates an organization-wide behavioral baseline — a competitive differentiator for grant applications and program evaluation.
